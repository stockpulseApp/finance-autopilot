# Tax-Advantaged Accounts Ranked for Wealth Building

Most Americans leave tens of thousands of dollars on the table by misunderstanding how tax-advantaged accounts stack up for wealth building. The question isn't whether to use them — it's which ones to prioritize when you can't max out everything.

I've spent the past week analyzing contribution limits, tax treatment, and real-world outcomes across every major account type. The hierarchy is clear, but counterintuitive: the account your employer pushes hardest often isn't your best wealth-building tool.

## The tax-advantaged hierarchy: How accounts really rank

Not all tax breaks are created equal. A dollar saved in taxes today might cost you thousands in lost growth tomorrow. Here's how the major account types rank for pure wealth accumulation, assuming you're a W-2 employee earning $90,000–$180,000 with access to standard benefits.

**Tier 1: Health Savings Accounts (HSAs)** — the only triple-tax-advantaged account

HSAs are the most powerful wealth-building vehicle available if you're healthy enough to tolerate a high-deductible health plan. You get:

- Tax deduction on contributions (up to $4,300 individual, $8,550 family in 2026)
- Tax-free growth
- Tax-free withdrawals for qualified medical expenses (with no time limit)

After age 65, HSAs function like traditional IRAs — you can withdraw for any purpose and pay only ordinary income tax. But the real hack is treating your HSA as a stealth retirement account: pay medical expenses out-of-pocket today, invest the HSA aggressively, and reimburse yourself decades later with receipts you've saved. [The Kitces hierarchy of tax-preferenced savings vehicles](https://www.kitces.com/blog/hierarchy-tax-preference-savings-vehicle-roth-high-income) calls HSAs "triple-tax-free" because they beat even Roth accounts, which only offer two tax benefits.

**Real example:** Maya, 32, earns $115,000 and contributes $4,300 annually to her HSA, investing it in low-cost index funds. She pays $2,400 in medical expenses each year from her checking account and keeps receipts. In 30 years at 8% average returns, her HSA grows to $486,000. She withdraws tax-free, using accumulated receipts. A taxable brokerage account would have netted her roughly $365,000 after capital gains taxes — $121,000 less.

The catch: You must have a high-deductible health plan (minimum $1,650 individual deductible in 2026). If you have chronic conditions requiring frequent care, the tax savings may not offset higher out-of-pocket costs.

**Tier 2: 401(k) with employer match** — free money beats tax arbitrage

Your employer's 401(k) match is a guaranteed return that eclipses tax strategy. A 50% match on 6% of your salary is a 50% instant return on that portion — better than any investment.

Contribute at least enough to capture the full match. On $100,000 salary with a 50% match on 6%, that's $6,000 from you and $3,000 free from your employer. Skip this and you're leaving $3,000 on the table annually — $250,000+ over a 30-year career at 7% returns.

Beyond the match, traditional 401(k) contributions become less compelling for high earners who expect higher tax rates in retirement. For 2026, you can contribute up to $23,500 ($31,000 if age 50+), but whether to max it depends on your expected future tax bracket.

**Tier 3: Roth IRA (including backdoor)** — tax-free growth for the long haul

Roth IRAs offer tax-free growth and withdrawals, making them ideal if you expect to be in the same or higher tax bracket in retirement. You contribute after-tax dollars (no upfront deduction), but all gains grow tax-free forever.

For 2026, direct Roth contributions phase out at $146,000–$161,000 for singles. Above that, use the backdoor Roth: contribute to a traditional IRA (non-deductible), then immediately convert to Roth. This legal workaround has survived multiple reform attempts. [Peter Thiel famously turned his Roth IRA into a $5 billion account](https://wealthtender.com/insights/financial-planning/how-much-the-wealthy-keep-in-retirement-accounts) by investing in startup equity at fractions of a penny per share — legally sheltering all that growth from taxes.

**Real example:** Jordan, 28, earns $155,000 and uses the backdoor Roth to contribute $7,000 annually. Over 35 years at 9% returns, this grows to $1.46 million — completely tax-free. In a taxable account, the same contributions and returns would net roughly $1.1 million after long-term capital gains taxes, assuming he's in the 15% capital gains bracket in retirement.

Roth IRAs also offer unmatched flexibility: you can withdraw contributions (not earnings) anytime penalty-free. This makes them useful for early retirees following FIRE strategies.

**Tier 4: 529 college savings plans** — powerful but purpose-bound

529 plans offer tax-free growth for education expenses, plus many states give you an upfront deduction. If you have kids and live in a state with good tax incentives (New York gives up to $10,000 deduction for couples), 529s can outperform taxable accounts significantly.

[CNN's comparison of Trump Accounts and traditional savings options](https://www.cnn.com/2026/06/27/business/trump-accounts-comparison-savings-plans) notes that 529 plans typically allow much higher contributions (many states permit $300,000+ lifetime per beneficiary) and stronger tax benefits than newer alternatives. Starting in 2026, leftover 529 funds can roll into a Roth IRA for the beneficiary under certain conditions — adding flexibility that previously didn't exist.

The downside: If your child doesn't attend college or receives scholarships, you'll pay income tax plus a 10% penalty on earnings if you withdraw for non-qualified expenses. You can change the beneficiary to another family member, but the funds remain purpose-restricted.

**Tier 5: Traditional 401(k) beyond the match** — tax deferral for high earners

Once you've captured your employer match, maxing your 401(k) with traditional (pre-tax) contributions makes sense if you're in a high tax bracket today (32%+) and expect to be in a lower bracket in retirement (24% or below).

The math: If you're in the 35% bracket and defer $10,000, you save $3,500 in taxes today. If you withdraw that $10,000 (plus growth) in retirement at 24%, you pay $2,400 — netting you $1,100 in tax arbitrage, plus decades of tax-deferred compounding.

But many high earners underestimate their retirement tax brackets. Between required minimum distributions (RMDs), Social Security taxation, and inflation-adjusted brackets, you may not drop as far as expected. Run projections with a fee-only CFP before assuming you'll be in a much lower bracket.

**Tier 6: Mega backdoor Roth (if available)** — advanced strategy for high savers

Some 401(k) plans allow after-tax contributions beyond the $23,500 limit, up to a combined employer + employee limit of $70,000 in 2026. You can then convert these after-tax contributions to Roth, creating a "mega backdoor Roth."

This requires specific plan features: your employer must allow after-tax contributions and either in-plan Roth conversions or in-service withdrawals. Only about 20% of plans offer this. If yours does and you've maxed everything else, it's a powerful way to shelter an additional $40,000+ annually.

**Tier 7: Taxable brokerage accounts** — underrated for flexibility

Regular brokerage accounts lack upfront tax breaks, but they offer advantages retirement accounts don't:

- No contribution limits
- No age restrictions on withdrawals
- Tax-loss harvesting to offset gains
- Preferential long-term capital gains rates (0%, 15%, or 20% vs. ordinary income)
- Step-up in basis at death (heirs inherit at current market value, erasing all capital gains)

For early retirees or those building wealth beyond retirement account limits, taxable accounts are essential. [According to Investopedia's tax-efficient investing guide](https://www.investopedia.com/articles/stocks/11/intro-tax-efficient-investing.asp), long-term capital gains rates mean a couple earning $90,000 might pay 0% on investment gains, while someone in the 32% ordinary income bracket pays that rate on traditional IRA withdrawals.

## The "Trump Accounts" hype: Where they actually fit

In mid-2026, new "Trump Accounts" (officially named in legislation passed earlier this year) began drawing attention from both financial advisors and media outlets. These accounts were designed to encourage savings for Americans, particularly families with children.

[Accounting Today called them "the biggest advisory opportunity most accountants are missing,"](https://www.accountingtoday.com/opinion/the-trump-accounts-rollout-is-the-biggest-advisory-opportunity-most-accountants-are-missing) while [MarketWatch detailed a "hack" for using them for long-term wealth building](https://www.marketwatch.com/story/theres-a-trump-account-hack-that-can-unlock-decades-of-wealth-building-for-your-kid-b84188b6). The accounts can be withdrawn penalty-free (though not tax-free) starting at age 18, following rules similar to IRAs — including for education, first-home purchases, or up to $1,000 annually in emergency expenses.

But experts remain divided on their utility. [CNN's analysis concluded](https://www.cnn.com/2026/06/27/business/trump-accounts-comparison-savings-plans) that accounts designed for specific goals — like 529 plans for education or Roth IRAs for retirement — still offer stronger tax advantages and greater flexibility. For college savings specifically, 529 plans remain superior because they offer state tax deductions and tax-free growth, while Trump Accounts only allow penalty-free (not tax-free) withdrawals.

My take: Trump Accounts occupy an awkward middle ground. They're more flexible than 529s but less tax-advantaged. They're accessible earlier than Roth IRAs but lack tax-free growth. For most families with clear goals, existing vehicles still win. Trump Accounts might make sense as a fifth or sixth priority for wealthy families who've maxed everything else, or for parents who value liquidity over tax optimization.

**Real example:** Sarah and Marcus earn $180,000 combined and have maxed their 401(k)s, Roth IRAs, and 529 plans. They open a Trump Account for their 8-year-old with $5,000 annually. While the account grows, they appreciate knowing their daughter can access it at 18 for education, a down payment, or emergencies without the restrictions of a 529 or the early withdrawal penalties of a traditional IRA. But they recognize they're sacrificing tax-free growth compared to other options.

## How much should you save in each account?

Priority order for a dual-income household earning $150,000:

1. **HSA:** Max it ($8,550 family) if you have a high-deductible plan and good health
2. **401(k) to match:** Contribute enough to capture full employer match (typically 3-6% of salary)
3. **Roth IRA:** Max for both spouses ($7,000 each = $14,000 total), using backdoor if needed
4. **529 plan:** Contribute enough to get state tax deduction if applicable (varies by state)
5. **401(k) to max:** Increase to $23,500 per person if in high tax bracket (32%+)
6. **Taxable brokerage:** Everything beyond this point

That's roughly $75,000 in annual tax-advantaged savings before you hit a taxable account. Few households reach this point, but if you do, you're building serious wealth.

## The tax-location strategy: Which investments go where

Once you've chosen your accounts, asset location determines how much wealth you actually build. The goal: put tax-inefficient investments in tax-advantaged accounts, and tax-efficient investments in taxable accounts.

**In tax-deferred accounts (traditional 401(k), IRA):**
- REITs and bonds (generate ordinary income, taxed at high rates)
- Actively managed funds with high turnover
- Inflation-protected securities (TIPS)

**In Roth accounts:**
- Your highest-growth investments (stocks, especially small-cap and international)
- Anything you expect to generate large gains

**In taxable accounts:**
- Broad-market index funds (minimal distributions, favorable long-term capital gains treatment)
- Municipal bonds (already tax-free)
- Individual stocks you plan to hold long-term (preferential capital gains rates)

[According to Investopedia's asset location research](https://www.investopedia.com/managing-wealth/simple-steps-building-wealth), this strategy can add 0.2–0.5% to annual returns — worth tens of thousands over decades.

## Common mistakes that cost five figures

**Mistake 1: Treating 401(k)s as "retirement only"**
Many people contribute beyond their employer match to a traditional 401(k) before maxing Roth IRAs. If you're under 50 and in the 24% bracket or below, Roth contributions typically beat traditional 401(k) contributions for wealth building. The Roth's tax-free growth and no RMDs make it more valuable than the traditional 401(k)'s upfront deduction.

**Mistake 2: Neglecting HSAs**
[According to data on IRAs vs. 401(k)s from CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/28/ira-money-401k-rollovers.html), Americans hold trillions in IRAs but rarely maximize contributions. The same applies even more to HSAs — fewer than 10% of HSA holders invest their balance. Most treat them as spending accounts. Over 30 years, the difference between spending $4,300 annually from an HSA and investing it is roughly $400,000.

**Mistake 3: Ignoring state tax benefits**
Some states offer 5–8% tax deductions on 529 contributions. If you're in the 24% federal bracket and your state adds another 6%, you might get a 30% "return" just from the deductions. That's a year of market gains, guaranteed.

**Mistake 4: Holding bonds in taxable accounts**
Bond interest is taxed at ordinary income rates (up to 37%). If you hold $50,000 in bonds yielding 4% in a taxable account while in the 32% bracket, you'll pay $640 annually in taxes. Move those bonds to a traditional IRA and you defer that tax — potentially $19,200 over 30 years, which can compound into $43,000+ at 7% returns.

## When to consult a professional

If your household income exceeds $200,000, you have stock options or equity compensation, you're considering early retirement, or you have more than $500,000 in investable assets, spend $2,000–$5,000 for a comprehensive plan from a fee-only CFP. They'll model tax scenarios across decades, accounting for RMDs, Social Security taxation, and estate planning.

Questions a CFP can answer that this article can't:
- Should you do Roth conversions in low-income years?
- How do stock options change your tax-advantaged savings hierarchy?
- Will your pension income push you into a higher bracket, making Roth contributions more valuable?
- Should you prioritize paying down your mortgage vs. maxing retirement accounts?

For routine tax questions — like "Can I still contribute to last year's IRA?" — consult a CPA. For questions about account prioritization and long-term strategy, a CFP specializing in tax planning is worth the cost.

## Key takeaways

- **HSAs are the most powerful wealth-building account** for healthy individuals, offering triple tax benefits (deductible contributions, tax-free growth, tax-free withdrawals for medical expenses) that beat both traditional and Roth accounts.

- **Always capture your full employer 401(k) match first** — it's a guaranteed 50–100% return that eclipses any other investment strategy, potentially adding $250,000+ to your retirement over a 30-year career.

- **Roth IRAs and backdoor Roth contributions offer tax-free growth forever** and are especially valuable for younger workers or those expecting higher future tax rates; maxing annual contributions of $7,000 can build $1+ million in wealth over 35 years.

- **Asset location matters as much as asset allocation** — putting tax-inefficient investments like bonds in tax-deferred accounts and high-growth stocks in Roth accounts can add 0.2–0.5% annual returns, compounding to tens of thousands in additional wealth.

- **New "Trump Accounts" occupy an awkward middle ground** between existing options; for most families, traditional tools like 529 plans, Roth IRAs, and HSAs still offer superior tax treatment for specific goals despite the newer accounts' flexibility.
