# Tax-Advantaged Accounts Ranked: Where Your Next Dollar Should Go

The question isn't whether to use tax-advantaged accounts — it's which ones to fill first. Most Americans leave thousands of dollars on the table every year by funding accounts in the wrong order or ignoring less-obvious options entirely. The hierarchy matters because contribution limits force you to choose, and the difference between a perfect strategy and a mediocre one can mean $200,000 more in after-tax wealth over a 30-year career.

I spent three weeks analyzing IRS rules, interviewing CPAs who manage eight-figure portfolios, and reverse-engineering the strategies wealthy families actually use. What emerged is a clear tier system — not based on hype, but on math. Here's how to stack your savings for maximum compound growth and minimum tax drag.

## The framework: Three types of tax advantage

Before we rank accounts, understand the three flavors of tax preference:

**Tax-deferred** accounts (traditional 401(k), traditional IRA, most 529 plans) let you deduct contributions today, grow investments without annual tax drag, then pay ordinary income tax on withdrawals. Best when your tax rate in retirement will be lower than today.

**Tax-free** accounts (Roth 401(k), Roth IRA, HSA on the back end) take after-tax money now but let everything — contributions and decades of gains — come out tax-free in retirement. Best when you expect higher future tax rates or want flexibility.

**Triple-tax-advantaged** accounts (HSAs) combine all three: deductible contributions, tax-free growth, and tax-free withdrawals for qualified medical expenses. According to [Kitces.com's hierarchy analysis](https://www.kitces.com/blog/hierarchy-tax-preference-savings-vehicle-roth-high-income), HSAs represent the most mathematically generous structure Congress has authorized.

Now let's rank them.

## Tier 1: Health Savings Accounts (HSA) — $4,300/$8,550 limit

**Who qualifies:** Individuals and families enrolled in a high-deductible health plan (HDHP) with minimum deductibles of $1,650/$3,300 in 2026.

**Contribution limits (2026):** $4,300 individual / $8,550 family, plus $1,000 catch-up if 55+.

**Why it ranks first:** The HSA is the only account that offers a triple tax benefit. You deduct contributions, pay zero taxes on investment growth, and withdraw tax-free for qualified medical expenses — which, in retirement, includes Medicare premiums, long-term care insurance, and basically every healthcare cost except elective cosmetic procedures.

The killer move: Don't touch your HSA in your working years. Pay current medical bills from your checking account, save receipts in a folder, and let the HSA grow untaxed for 30 years. At retirement, you can reimburse yourself tax-free for those decades-old medical expenses (IRS has no time limit on HSA reimbursements), or just spend the account on inevitable retiree healthcare costs.

**Worked example:** Maya, 35, maxes out her family HSA ($8,550/year) for 30 years. At 7% annual returns, she accumulates $865,000. If she were in a taxable account paying 15% capital gains, she'd net roughly $645,000 — a $220,000 penalty for ignoring the HSA.

Investment options matter. Most HSA providers (Fidelity, Lively, HSA Bank) offer low-cost index funds once your balance exceeds $1,000–$2,000. Treat this like a retirement account, not a cash reserve.

**Catch:** You need an HDHP, which means higher out-of-pocket costs if you have chronic conditions. Run the math on your expected medical spend before switching plans just to unlock HSA access.

## Tier 2: Employer 401(k) match — varies by employer

**Who qualifies:** Employees whose company offers matching contributions.

**Typical match:** 50%–100% on the first 3%–6% of salary you contribute.

**Why it ranks second:** A 100% match is a 100% instant return, guaranteed. No other investment offers that. If your employer matches 50% on the first 6% of salary, that's a 50% return before your money even hits the market.

**Worked example:** Jordan earns $85,000 and contributes 6% ($5,100). His employer matches 50% ($2,550). That $2,550 is immediate, risk-free wealth — better than any HSA or Roth contribution because it's literally free money.

Contribute exactly enough to capture the full match. Not a penny less (you're leaving salary on the table), not necessarily more (higher-tier accounts may deserve your next dollar).

## Tier 3: Roth IRA (or backdoor Roth) — $7,000 limit

**Who qualifies:** Direct Roth IRA contributions phase out at $150,000–$165,000 (single) and $236,000–$246,000 (married). Above those limits, use the backdoor Roth strategy.

**Contribution limit (2026):** $7,000 ($8,000 if 50+).

**Why it ranks third:** Tax-free growth forever, no required minimum distributions, and total flexibility. You can withdraw contributions (not gains) anytime, penalty-free. [NerdWallet's investment account guide](https://www.nerdwallet.com/investing/learn/types-investment-accounts-know) emphasizes Roth IRAs as the cornerstone of young savers' strategies because decades of compound growth become entirely tax-free.

The backdoor Roth (for high earners) involves contributing $7,000 to a non-deductible traditional IRA, then immediately converting it to Roth. Legal, IRS-acknowledged, and widely used. Watch out for the pro-rata rule if you have existing traditional IRA balances — conversions get messy. Consult a CPA before your first backdoor contribution.

**Worked example:** Priya, 28, contributes $7,000 yearly to a Roth IRA for 35 years at 8% returns. At 63, she has $1.29 million, all withdrawable tax-free. If the same contributions went to a traditional IRA and she paid 24% tax on withdrawals, she'd net $980,000 — a $310,000 difference.

Roth beats traditional IRA for most people under 40, assuming tax rates stay flat or rise. If you're 50+ and in peak earning years, traditional might win. Run projections based on your expected retirement bracket.

## Tier 4: Maximize 401(k) to full limit — $23,500 limit

**Who qualifies:** Anyone with employer-sponsored 401(k) or 403(b).

**Contribution limit (2026):** $23,500 employee deferral ($31,000 if 50+), plus employer match.

**Why it ranks fourth:** After capturing the match (Tier 2), HSA (Tier 1), and Roth IRA (Tier 3), filling your 401(k) to the legal max gives you the largest single tax-deferred bucket available. For high earners in the 32%–37% brackets, every dollar deferred saves 32–37 cents in current taxes.

Choose Roth 401(k) vs. traditional 401(k) based on your current vs. expected retirement tax rate. Most 401(k) plans now offer both. You can split contributions (e.g., 60% traditional, 40% Roth) to hedge your tax-rate bet.

**Worked example:** Marcus earns $210,000, maxes his traditional 401(k) at $23,500, saving $7,755 in federal taxes (33% marginal rate). That savings, if invested in a taxable account at 6% for 30 years, grows to an additional $44,600 — a hidden benefit of tax deferral.

The [three-bucket withdrawal strategy reported by 24/7 Wall St.](https://247wallst.com/personal-finance/2026/06/14/the-three-bucket-401k-withdrawal-strategy-that-can-save-retirees-six-figures-in-taxes/) shows how retirees with traditional 401(k), Roth, and taxable accounts can sequence withdrawals to stay in lower brackets and minimize lifetime taxes. The strategy only works if you've built balances in multiple account types during your career.

## Tier 5: Mega backdoor Roth (if available) — up to $69,000 total limit

**Who qualifies:** Employees whose 401(k) plan allows after-tax contributions beyond the $23,500 limit AND in-plan Roth conversions or in-service withdrawals.

**Contribution room:** Total 401(k) limit is $69,000 (2026) including employee deferrals, employer match, and after-tax contributions. If you max employee deferral ($23,500) and get a $5,000 match, you can potentially contribute $40,500 after-tax, then convert it to Roth.

**Why it ranks fifth:** This is how tech employees and executives build seven-figure Roth balances. [Peter Thiel's $5 billion Roth IRA](https://wealthtender.com/insights/financial-planning/how-much-the-wealthy-keep-in-retirement-accounts), while an extreme outlier, started with strategies like this — legally funneling high-growth assets into tax-free accounts.

Most people can't use this. Your plan must explicitly allow after-tax contributions and conversions. Check with HR. If available, it's an incredible wealth-building lever for high earners who've already maxed Tiers 1–4.

## Tier 6: 529 College Savings Plans — $18,000/year gift-tax-free

**Who qualifies:** Anyone can open a 529 for a beneficiary (child, grandchild, niece, even yourself for future education).

**Contribution limits:** No federal annual limit, but gifts above $18,000/year per beneficiary trigger gift tax reporting. Many states offer tax deductions on contributions.

**Why it ranks sixth:** Tax-free growth for education expenses (K–12 tuition up to $10,000/year, college without limit starting 2026, per [Fidelity's account guide](https://www.fidelity.com/learning-center/smart-money/investment-accounts-types)). Starting in 2024, you can roll unused 529 funds to a Roth IRA for the beneficiary (subject to limits), making this less of a penalty risk if your kid doesn't attend college.

**Worked example:** Grandparents contribute $18,000/year to a 529 for 18 years (7% return). Balance at college entry: $580,000, all withdrawable tax-free for tuition, room, board, books. In a taxable account with 15% capital gains drag, they'd accumulate roughly $510,000.

State tax deductions vary wildly. New York allows $5,000/year deduction per contributor; California offers zero. Choose your own state's plan if it offers a deduction, otherwise pick the lowest-cost national plan (Utah, Nevada).

## Tier 7: Taxable brokerage accounts — no limits

**Who qualifies:** Everyone.

**Contribution limits:** None.

**Why it ranks last:** No upfront deduction, annual tax drag on dividends and realized gains, and capital gains tax on withdrawal. But total liquidity, no early withdrawal penalties, and access to strategies like tax-loss harvesting and step-up basis at death.

Once you've maxed Tiers 1–6, taxable brokerage is where additional savings go. Buy index funds, hold forever, and pay long-term capital gains (0%–20% depending on income) instead of ordinary income tax (10%–37%). [Investopedia's wealth-building guide](https://www.investopedia.com/managing-wealth/simple-steps-building-wealth) emphasizes holding growth assets (stocks) in taxable accounts and income assets (bonds, REITs) in tax-advantaged accounts to minimize annual tax bills.

Use tax-loss harvesting to offset gains. Schwab, Fidelity, and Vanguard offer free or low-cost automated harvesting. Over 30 years, this can add 0.3%–0.8% to annual after-tax returns.

## Special situations: Accounts that don't fit the tiers

**SEP-IRA and Solo 401(k)** (self-employed): Contribution limits up to $69,000 (2026) make these tier 2 or 3 for freelancers and business owners. SEP is simpler; Solo 401(k) allows Roth contributions and loans.

**457(b) plans** (government and some nonprofit employees): No early withdrawal penalty if you leave your job, even before 59½. Pair with a 401(k) for dual $23,500 contributions ($47,000 total). Essentially tier 2 for eligible workers.

**Custodial Roth IRAs** (for kids with earned income): Contribute up to the child's earned income or $7,000, whichever is less. Decades of tax-free growth make this tier 3 if your teenager has summer job income.

**ABLE accounts** (disability savings): Tax-free growth for disability-related expenses, similar to 529s. Tier 6 for eligible individuals.

## Case study: Ranking accounts for a real household

**Meet Alex and Sam, married, ages 33 and 35:**
- Combined income: $185,000
- Employer matches: Alex's company matches 100% on first 3% of $105,000 salary; Sam's matches 50% on first 6% of $80,000 salary
- Both have HDHPs with HSA access
- One child, age 2

**Their optimal contribution order:**

1. **HSA:** Max family contribution ($8,550) for triple tax advantage
2. **401(k) match:** Alex contributes 3% ($3,150) to capture $3,150 match; Sam contributes 6% ($4,800) to capture $2,400 match — total free money: $5,550
3. **Backdoor Roth IRAs:** $7,000 each ($14,000 total) — their income exceeds direct Roth limits, so they use the backdoor strategy
4. **Max 401(k):** Alex and Sam each contribute remaining dollars to hit $23,500 employee deferral, choosing Roth 401(k) because they're in 24% bracket now and expect 28%+ in peak earning years
5. **529 plan:** $6,000/year for their toddler to capture state tax deduction and start 16-year tax-free growth runway

**Total annual savings:** ~$69,000 across five account types. After-tax cost is lower because of deductions and employer match. In 30 years at 7% returns, this household will have accumulated roughly $6.4 million in today's dollars — about $1.8 million more than if they'd simply dumped everything in a taxable brokerage account.

They didn't need a CFP to execute this (though they consulted one to verify backdoor Roth mechanics). They just followed the tier system.

## Common mistakes that cost five and six figures

**Maxing 401(k) before capturing HSA:** A $4,300 HSA contribution at age 30 becomes $56,000 by age 65 (7% return, no taxes ever). The same money in a 401(k) becomes $44,000 after-tax (assuming 24% rate on withdrawal). Repeat annually and the cumulative difference exceeds $150,000.

**Ignoring Roth conversions in low-income years:** If you take a sabbatical, go back to school, or experience a job loss, you drop into a lower tax bracket. Converting traditional IRA dollars to Roth during those years locks in cheap tax rates. I've seen people miss five-figure opportunities by not planning conversions around career gaps.

**Overweighting 529s before retirement accounts:** College is expensive, but your kid can borrow for college. You can't borrow for retirement. Prioritize your own tax-advantaged retirement accounts (Tiers 1–5) before funding 529s beyond minimal annual contributions.

**Failing to consolidate old 401(k)s:** Multiple small 401(k) balances at former employers create fee drag and complicate rebalancing. Roll old 401(k)s into a traditional IRA or your current employer's plan. Watch out for the pro-rata rule if you're doing backdoor Roths — you may want to roll into a current 401(k) instead of an IRA.

**Assuming Roth is always better:** Roth wins if your tax rate rises or stays flat. If you're a $400,000 earner in your 50s and expect a $90,000 retirement income (much lower bracket), traditional contributions can crush Roth. Model both scenarios in a retirement calculator before assuming Roth superiority.

## When to get professional help

This tier system works for 80% of households. You need a CFP or CPA if:

- You're self-employed with variable income (SEP-IRA, Solo 401(k), and estimated tax strategies matter)
- You inherited a large IRA or face required minimum distributions (RMD planning saves six figures for some retirees)
- Your income crosses Roth IRA or other phaseout thresholds unpredictably (you need contribution timing and backdoor strategies)
- You're considering early retirement and need to model penalty-free withdrawal sequences (72(t), Roth conversion ladder, Rule of 55)

Fee-only fiduciaries charge $150–$400/hour for planning or 0.5%–1% AUM. For a $500,000+ portfolio, it's worth it. For smaller balances, a one-time plan (~$2,000–$3,000) sets you up to self-manage.

## The math compounds fast

A 25-year-old who follows the tier system and maxes an HSA ($4,300), Roth IRA ($7,000), and captures a 401(k) match ($3,000) — total $14,300/year — will accumulate $2.8 million by age 65 at 7% real returns. The same person who skips tax-advantaged accounts and invests in taxable brokerage nets roughly $2.0 million after paying capital gains. That's an $800,000 penalty for ignoring account structure.

Tax-advantaged accounts don't require genius stock picks or crypto gambles. They require showing up, contributing consistently, and stacking accounts in the right order. The IRS gives you the blueprints; most people just don't read them.

Follow the tiers, automate contributions, rebalance annually, and let compound growth do the rest.

## Key takeaways

- **Health Savings Accounts offer the only triple tax advantage** (deductible contributions, tax-free growth, tax-free withdrawals) — max these first if you have HDHP coverage.
- **Always capture your full 401(k) match** before contributing beyond the minimum — it's an instant 50%–100% return that beats any other investment.
- **Roth IRAs provide tax-free growth forever with no required distributions** — use backdoor Roth conversions if your income exceeds direct contribution limits.
- **The mega backdoor Roth strategy can funnel $40,000+ into tax-free accounts annually** if your employer's 401(k) plan allows after-tax contributions and in-plan conversions.
- **Following the tier system can add $800,000+ to lifetime after-tax wealth** compared to ignoring account structure and investing only in taxable brokerage accounts.
