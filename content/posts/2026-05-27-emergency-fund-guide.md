## Why an emergency fund comes first

Before you chase high returns, you need a buffer against surprises: job loss, medical bills, car repairs, or a leaky roof. An emergency fund is **insurance for your cash flow**, not an investment.

## The sizing framework

| Situation | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Stable job, dual income | 3 months of essential expenses |
| Single income or variable pay | 6 months |
| Self-employed or commission-heavy | 9–12 months |

**Essential expenses** means housing, utilities, food, insurance, minimum debt payments, and transport — not dining out or subscriptions you would cut in a crisis.

## Where to park it

- **High-yield savings account** — FDIC insured, liquid, competitive APY.
- **Money market fund** (in a brokerage) — slightly higher yield, still accessible.

Avoid stocks, crypto, or long-term CDs for your core emergency money.

## How to build it on autopilot

1. Set a monthly transfer the day after payday.
2. Use a separate account so you are not tempted to spend it.
3. Pause extra investing until you hit **one month** of expenses, then split: 50% to the fund, 50% to investments until you are fully funded.

## Common mistakes

- Keeping the fund in checking (too easy to spend).
- Calling a vacation fund an "emergency" fund.
- Investing emergency money for yield and selling at a loss when you need cash.

## Bottom line

Size the fund to your risk, automate contributions, and only then lean hard into long-term investing. That order protects both your peace of mind and your portfolio.
