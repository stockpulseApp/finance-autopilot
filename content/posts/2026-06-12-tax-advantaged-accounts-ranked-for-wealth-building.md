# Tax-Advantaged Accounts Ranked: Which Ones Actually Build the Most Wealth?

Every dollar you earn faces a choice: get taxed now, get taxed later, or never get taxed at all. The third option sounds mythical, but it exists — and understanding the hierarchy of tax-advantaged accounts can mean the difference between retiring comfortably at 62 or working until 70.

Tax-advantaged accounts aren't all created equal. Some save you 22% on every dollar. Others save 22% plus 7.65% FICA plus state tax plus eliminate capital gains forever. The gap between optimal and mediocre account selection compounds to six figures over a career.

This isn't theory. Meet Sarah Chen, a 34-year-old software engineer in Seattle earning $145,000. She has $2,000 monthly to invest beyond her emergency fund. If she defaults to a standard brokerage account, she'll pay Washington's new 7% capital gains tax, 15–20% federal capital gains, and 3.8% net investment income tax on growth. But if she sequences her accounts strategically, she can shield that same $2,000 from all three — and pick up an employer match and a deduction to boot.

The question isn't whether to use tax-advantaged accounts. It's which ones, in what order, and how much.

## The three flavors of tax advantage

Tax-advantaged accounts operate on three timing models, each with different math:

**Tax-deferred accounts** (traditional 401(k), traditional IRA, SEP-IRA) give you a deduction today. If you're in the 24% federal bracket, a $6,500 IRA contribution cuts your tax bill by $1,560. The money grows without annual tax drag, but you pay ordinary income tax on every dollar you withdraw in retirement. The bet: your retirement tax rate will be lower than today's.

**Tax-free accounts** (Roth IRA, Roth 401(k), Roth conversions) take after-tax dollars but never tax the growth or withdrawals. A $7,000 Roth IRA contribution at age 30 that grows to $56,000 by age 65 (assuming 7% returns) produces $49,000 of gain you'll never pay tax on. The bet: tax rates will rise, or your retirement income will land you in a higher bracket than expected.

**Triple-tax-advantaged accounts** (Health Savings Accounts) offer the unicorn: deductible contributions, tax-free growth, and tax-free withdrawals for qualified medical expenses. That's a 22% deduction going in, zero tax on decades of compounding, and zero tax coming out. No other account does this.

The hierarchy matters because contribution limits force tradeoffs. You can't max out everything, so you need a decision tree.

## Tier 1: Health Savings Accounts — the undisputed champion

If you're eligible for an HSA (meaning you have a qualifying high-deductible health plan with a deductible of at least $1,650 for individuals or $3,300 for families in 2026), this is your first dollar of savings. Period.

2026 contribution limits: $4,300 individual, $8,550 family, plus $1,000 catch-up if you're 55+.

The triple tax advantage is absurd. A $4,300 contribution at age 35 saves $1,032 in federal tax if you're in the 24% bracket — plus another $329 in FICA if contributed through payroll. That's $1,361 in year-one tax savings on a $4,300 deposit, a 32% immediate return before any investment growth.

Invest the HSA in a low-cost stock index fund (many custodians now offer this once your cash balance exceeds $1,000–2,000), let it compound for 30 years, and you have a pile of money that comes out tax-free for medical expenses. Given that the average retired couple will spend $315,000 on healthcare in retirement (Fidelity 2025 estimate), you'll have qualified expenses. [Fidelity Investments notes](https://www.fidelity.com/learning-center/smart-money/average-net-worth-by-age) that employer-sponsored HSAs often include employer contributions, amplifying the benefit.

**The catch:** You must have a high-deductible health plan, which means higher out-of-pocket costs if you need care. For healthy young people or those with predictable low medical costs, this trade works. For someone managing chronic conditions, it may not.

**Sarah's move:** She switches to her employer's HDHP and contributes the full $4,300 to an HSA via payroll deduction. She invests it in VTSAX and doesn't touch it — she pays current medical bills from her checking account and saves receipts. In 30 years, that $4,300 (if she never adds another dollar) could grow to $32,800 at 7% returns, all accessible tax-free.

## Tier 2: Employer 401(k) match — free money with a vesting countdown

Your employer match is a guaranteed 50–100% return. If your company matches 50% of the first 6% of your salary, and you earn $145,000, that's $4,350 of free money annually. Not taking it is setting $4,350 on fire.

Contribute enough to get the full match, even if you don't max out the 401(k). This goes in either Roth or traditional flavor, depending on your tax situation (see Tier 5).

**The catch:** Vesting schedules. Many employers require 2–4 years before the match is fully yours. If you job-hop, you forfeit unvested dollars. Also, 401(k) fees vary wildly. If your plan charges 1%+ in fund expense ratios, you're donating thousands to financial services firms over a career.

**Sarah's move:** Her employer matches 100% of the first 3%, so she contributes $4,350 to get the $4,350 match. That's $8,700 working for her. She chooses Roth 401(k) because she expects her income (and tax rates) to rise.

## Tier 3: Roth IRA (or Backdoor Roth) — tax-free growth for life

Roth IRAs are funded with after-tax dollars, offer tax-free growth, and [CNBC reports](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/05/trump-accounts-vs-529s-roth-iras.html) that they require no required minimum distributions (RMDs) in retirement, making them ideal for wealth transfer and late-life flexibility.

2026 contribution limit: $7,000, or $8,000 if you're 50+.

**The catch:** Income phaseouts. If you're single and earn over $165,000 (or married filing jointly over $246,000), you can't contribute directly. But you can use the Backdoor Roth: contribute $7,000 to a traditional IRA (non-deductible), then immediately convert to Roth. There's no income limit on conversions, only contributions.

The Backdoor Roth has one landmine: the pro-rata rule. If you have existing pre-tax IRA balances (like an old rollover IRA from a previous 401(k)), part of your conversion will be taxable. The fix: roll old IRA money into your current 401(k) before doing the Backdoor, or accept the partial tax hit.

**Sarah's move:** She earns $145,000, so she can contribute directly. She puts $7,000 into a Roth IRA at Vanguard in a target-date fund. Over 30 years at 7%, that becomes $53,400 — and the $46,400 of gain is tax-free.

## Tier 4: Max out the 401(k) — traditional or Roth, depending on your bracket

Once you've captured the match, filled your HSA, and maxed your Roth IRA, the next frontier is maxing the 401(k).

2026 employee contribution limit: $23,500 (plus $7,500 catch-up if you're 50+).

Should you go traditional (pre-tax) or Roth (after-tax)? The conventional wisdom: if you're in the 22% bracket or lower today and expect to be in the 12% or lower bracket in retirement, go traditional. If you're in 24%+ today and expect tax rates to rise or your retirement income to be high (pensions, rental income, large RMDs), go Roth.

Reality is messier. Traditional contributions reduce your AGI, which can help you qualify for other tax credits (like the Child Tax Credit or student loan interest deduction). Roth contributions don't lower your current tax bill, but they create a bucket of money exempt from future RMDs, giving you more control over taxable income at age 73+.

**Sarah's move:** She's already contributing $4,350 to get the match. She decides to contribute another $10,000 in Roth 401(k) contributions (total $14,350). She's in the 24% federal + 10.23% Seattle payroll tax bracket, so this costs her real money today — but she's 34, expects tech income to grow, and wants tax-free money in 30 years when tax rates may be higher.

## Tier 5: Mega Backdoor Roth — if your plan allows after-tax contributions

Some 401(k) plans allow after-tax (non-Roth) contributions beyond the $23,500 employee limit, up to the overall 2026 limit of $70,000 (including employer contributions). You contribute after-tax dollars, then immediately convert them to Roth via an in-plan conversion or rollover to a Roth IRA.

This is the Mega Backdoor Roth, and it's legal if your plan supports it. Many don't. Call your benefits department and ask: "Does our 401(k) allow after-tax contributions and in-service Roth conversions?"

If yes, you can funnel an extra $30,000–40,000 into Roth money annually, depending on your salary and employer contributions. That's how high earners in tech and finance build seven-figure Roth balances before 50.

**The catch:** Complex administration, requires frequent conversions to avoid taxable gains, and not available in most plans.

**Sarah's move:** Her employer plan doesn't offer this. She moves on.

## Tier 6: 529 plans — state tax deduction plus tax-free growth for education

If you have kids (or plan to), 529 college savings plans offer state tax deductions (in most states) on contributions and federal tax-free growth if spent on qualified education expenses.

Many states offer deductions of $2,000–10,000 per year. New York allows up to $10,000 married filing jointly; California offers no deduction but provides tax-free growth.

The SECURE 2.0 Act (2022) now allows up to $35,000 of unused 529 money to be rolled into the beneficiary's Roth IRA over their lifetime, subject to annual Roth contribution limits and a 15-year account age requirement. This reduces the "what if my kid doesn't go to college" risk.

[CNBC notes](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/05/trump-accounts-vs-529s-roth-iras.html) that 529 plans are often the most tax-advantageous option for education savings, particularly for high earners in states with generous deductions.

**The catch:** Money must be used for qualified education expenses (tuition, room, board, books) or you pay tax + 10% penalty on gains. Recent expansions allow up to $10,000 for K–12 tuition and student loan repayment, but limits apply.

**Sarah's move:** No kids yet. She skips this for now but notes it for future years.

## Tier 7: Taxable brokerage — the flexibility account

Once you've maxed tax-advantaged space, additional savings go into a standard brokerage account. You pay tax on dividends and capital gains annually, but you also get:

- No contribution limits
- No age restrictions on withdrawals
- No required minimum distributions
- Access to tax-loss harvesting to offset gains
- Step-up in basis at death (heirs inherit at market value, erasing capital gains)

For early retirees or people building wealth beyond retirement, taxable accounts are essential. You can't live on 401(k) money before 59½ without penalties (barring 72(t) distributions or Roth conversion ladders, both complex).

**Tax efficiency matters here.** Hold individual stocks you plan to keep 1+ years, index funds, ETFs, and municipal bonds in taxable. Hold actively managed funds, REITs, and high-yield bonds in tax-advantaged accounts. [Charles Schwab advises](https://www.schwab.com/learn/story/tax-efficient-investing-why-is-it-important) that tax-managed funds and low-turnover index funds are ideal for taxable accounts, while high-turnover strategies belong in IRAs.

**Sarah's move:** After funding HSA ($4,300), 401(k) Roth ($14,350), and Roth IRA ($7,000), she has $2,000/month = $24,000/year to deploy. She's committed $25,650 to tax-advantaged accounts, leaving nothing for taxable this year. Next year, after a raise, she'll open a taxable account at Fidelity and buy VTI.

## The accounts that didn't make the top tier

**Deferred compensation plans (457, non-qualified deferred comp):** Available to government workers and some executives. 457(b) plans are similar to 401(k)s but allow penalty-free withdrawals before 59½ if you separate from service. Non-qualified plans carry creditor risk (you're an unsecured creditor if the company goes bankrupt). Use only after maxing other options.

**SEP-IRA and Solo 401(k):** For self-employed people. SEP-IRAs allow up to 25% of net self-employment income (max $70,000 in 2026). Solo 401(k)s allow $23,500 employee contribution plus up to 25% employer contribution (same $70,000 total cap). Solo 401(k) wins because it allows Roth contributions and loans.

**Traditional IRA (without employer plan):** If you're not covered by a workplace retirement plan, you can deduct traditional IRA contributions regardless of income. But if you or your spouse have a 401(k), income limits apply ($87,000–$107,000 single for partial deduction in 2026). Most high earners can't deduct, making Backdoor Roth the better move.

**Gold IRAs and alternative asset IRAs:** [CNBC mentions](https://www.cnbc.com/select/the-price-of-gold-today-june-10-2026/) that gold IRAs allow you to hold IRS-approved precious metals with tax benefits, but they carry setup fees, storage fees, and custodian fees that erode returns. Unless you have a strong conviction that gold will outperform equities by enough to overcome 1–2% annual fees, skip this.

**Trump Accounts:** A new initiative allowing parents to open investment accounts for children with seed money. Details are still emerging, and [Investor.gov references](https://www.sec.gov/investor/seniors.shtml) these accounts alongside traditional 529 plans and IRAs as tax-advantaged options. The CNBC article notes that over 5 million children have been enrolled, with 1.2 million eligible for $1,000 in seed funding. However, without clear long-term data on tax treatment, contribution limits, and withdrawal rules, it's premature to rank these against established accounts. Worth watching, not yet essential.

## The sequencing that actually matters

Here's the decision tree for Sarah and anyone else with limited dollars to deploy:

1. **HSA to the max** ($4,300 individual) — if eligible
2. **401(k) to the match** — whatever gets you full employer contribution
3. **Roth IRA to the max** ($7,000) — or Backdoor Roth if over income limits
4. **401(k) to the max** ($23,500 total) — Roth or traditional depending on bracket
5. **Mega Backdoor Roth** — if plan allows after-tax contributions
6. **529 plan** — if you have kids and your state offers a deduction
7. **Taxable brokerage** — for everything else

This isn't universal. If you're 55 and in the 12% bracket with a pension, traditional 401(k) beats Roth. If you're 28 in the 32% bracket expecting income growth, Roth wins. If you're self-employed, Solo 401(k) leapfrogs standard 401(k). If you're planning early retirement at 50, you need taxable money to bridge the gap to 59½.

But the hierarchy gives you a starting point. Most people don't fail because they chose Roth over traditional. They fail because they saved 3% in a 401(k) and spent the rest.

## The math on a 30-year horizon

Return to Sarah. She's 34, earns $145,000, and commits to this annual sequence:

- HSA: $4,300
- 401(k) Roth: $14,350 (including $4,350 match)
- Roth IRA: $7,000
- Taxable brokerage: $5,000 (starting year 2 after a raise)

**Total saved annually:** $30,650

Assuming 7% real returns (after inflation), here's where she lands at age 64:

- **HSA:** $435,000 (tax-free for medical expenses)
- **401(k) Roth:** $1,450,000 (tax-free withdrawals)
- **Roth IRA:** $707,000 (tax-free withdrawals)
- **Taxable brokerage:** $505,000 (taxed at long-term capital gains rates, likely 15%)

**Total:** $3,097,000

If she had put everything in a taxable account instead, she'd have roughly $2,400,000 after paying annual tax drag on dividends and a 15% capital gains bite on $1,800,000 of gains at withdrawal — a $697,000 difference. That's a year of early retirement, a second home, or generational wealth for her kids.

The tax-advantaged account hierarchy isn't a parlor trick. It's the difference between financial independence and working into your 70s.

## When to consult a professional

This article is educational, not personalized advice. Tax rules change, income limits adjust annually, and your situation (state taxes, employer plan features, estate planning goals, early retirement plans) introduces variables this piece can't address.

Consult a fee-only CFP® if you're navigating:

- Backdoor Roth with existing IRA balances (pro-rata rule)
- Mega Backdoor Roth setup and execution
- Roth conversion strategies in early retirement
- Coordination between 529s, Roth IRAs, and custodial accounts for kids
- Self-employment retirement plan selection (SEP vs. Solo 401(k) vs. defined benefit)
  
Consult a CPA if you're facing:

- State tax implications of account selection (some states tax Roth contributions, others don't)
- Non-qualified deferred comp elections
- Early withdrawal penalties and exceptions
- RMD calculations and strategies to minimize them

## Key takeaways

- **HSAs are the single best tax-advantaged account** — triple tax benefit (deductible, tax-free growth, tax-free withdrawals) if you're eligible with a high-deductible health plan.

- **Always capture the full 401(k) match** — it's a guaranteed 50–100% return; contributing less is leaving money on the table.

- **Roth IRAs and Roth 401(k)s offer tax-free growth** with no RMDs, making them ideal for young earners, those expecting higher future tax rates, or anyone wanting flexible estate planning.

- **The Backdoor Roth and Mega Backdoor Roth let high earners access Roth benefits** despite income limits, but require careful execution to avoid tax traps like the pro-rata rule.

- **Sequencing matters more than account selection** — maxing out tax-advantaged space in the optimal order can add six figures to your net worth over a career compared to random contributions or taxable-only investing.
