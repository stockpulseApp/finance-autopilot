# Dollar-cost averaging when markets feel scary

The S&P 500 sits near all-time highs. Middle East tensions flare. Bond supply worries analysts. Your portfolio statement shows gains you never expected to see—and suddenly every headline feels like a warning bell.

So you freeze. You stop contributing to your 401(k). You tell yourself you'll start investing again "when things calm down." And just like that, you've stopped following the one investing process that actually protects you when you have no idea what comes next.

Dollar-cost averaging (DCA)—the practice of investing fixed dollar amounts at regular intervals—sounds almost embarrassingly simple. That simplicity is precisely why it works when your gut screams to do something, anything, other than buy stocks that already feel expensive.

But here's what most articles on DCA miss: the real challenge isn't understanding the math. It's continuing to execute when the market feels scary, when every instinct tells you to wait, and when smart-sounding people predict a crash.

## The emotional trap disguised as logic

Market strategist commentary from [KITCO: When the Market Rises, and You're Positioned for the Fall](https://www.kitco.com/opinion/2026-07-17/when-market-rises-and-youre-positioned-fall) nails the core problem: "Most investors do not get into trouble because they are trying to make poor decisions. They get into trouble because emotion can disguise itself as logic."

A market near highs makes defensive positioning feel prudent. A geopolitical headline makes cash feel responsible. A bearish forecast from a credentialed analyst makes hedging feel intelligent.

None of these are investment processes. They're emotional responses wearing a logic costume.

Consider Maya Chen, 34, earning $78,000 as a marketing manager in Austin. She started her Roth IRA in January 2023, contributing $500 monthly into a target-date fund. By July 2024, her account had grown to $11,200. Then she read three articles about "bubble valuations" in tech stocks, saw the VIX spike above 20, and decided to "pause contributions until things settle down."

Eighteen months later, she's still paused. Her account sits at $14,100—not because the market crashed, but because it kept climbing while she waited for the perfect entry point. She missed $9,000 in contributions and the gains on those dollars. The market never felt safe enough.

This is the real cost of letting fear masquerade as prudence.

## How dollar-cost averaging actually protects you

DCA doesn't prevent losses. It removes the need to make an impossible prediction about market timing.

When you commit to investing the same dollar amount each period—say, $500 every payday—you automatically buy fewer shares when prices are high and more shares when prices drop. The math is mechanical:

- Month 1: $500 buys 10 shares at $50
- Month 2: $500 buys 8.33 shares at $60 (market up 20%)
- Month 3: $500 buys 12.5 shares at $40 (market down 33%)

After three months, you've invested $1,500 and own 30.83 shares. Your average cost per share is $48.66—less than two of the three purchase prices.

Now compare that to Maya's approach: waiting for the "right" time. She would have tried to call the top in month 2 and the bottom in month 3. Research shows that professional money managers consistently fail at this. Retail investors fare worse.

The [Motley Fool: "It's Gambling": Warren Buffett Just Issued a Blunt Warning to Investors](https://www.fool.com/investing/2026/07/19/its-gambling-warren-buffett-just-issued-a-blunt-wa/) reminds us that Buffett himself has repeatedly warned against trying to time markets. His advice for regular investors? Consistent, automatic contributions to low-cost index funds.

DCA doesn't require you to be smarter than the market. It requires you to be more disciplined than your emotions.

## When scary markets are actually DCA's best friend

Volatility feels terrible. It's also when dollar-cost averaging delivers its greatest mathematical advantage.

Take James Martinez, 41, an insurance adjuster in Phoenix earning $64,000. He contributes $400 monthly to his 401(k), split between a U.S. stock index fund and an international fund. In October 2024, markets dropped 8% in three weeks on concerns about commercial real estate exposure at regional banks.

James's October contribution bought 8.7% more shares than his September contribution. When markets recovered by January 2025, those "cheap" shares generated the same gains as everything else in his portfolio—but he owned more of them.

He didn't need to predict the bottom. He didn't need to increase his contribution (though that would have helped). He simply kept the same $400 monthly schedule running, and the math took care of the rest.

[CNBC: The worst dotcom bubble investing mistakes are coming for your portfolio](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/21/market-bubble-stock-risks-investing-mistakes.html) warns that single-sector bets and attempts to ride momentum often end badly. The article quotes Neale Ellis, founding partner at Fidelis Capital: "I would never just own one sector ETF because you could be wrong."

DCA naturally hedges against this mistake. When you contribute monthly to a diversified index fund, you're not betting on any single sector or timing decision. You're betting that over decades, productive businesses will generally create value—a bet that has paid off in every historical 30-year period.

## The math behind buying expensive shares

Here's the objection everyone raises: "Why would I keep buying when the market is obviously expensive?"

Because you don't actually know if it's expensive relative to future prices.

The S&P 500 hit an all-time high in January 2018. Investors who stopped contributing because "valuations looked stretched" missed the 2019 gains (31.5%), and if they stayed out, they missed 2024's recovery and 2025's gains. Yes, 2020 and 2022 had corrections—but DCA investors bought those dips automatically.

Someone who contributed $500 monthly from January 2018 through December 2025 invested $48,000. Despite buying through record highs in 2018, the 2020 COVID crash, the 2022 bear market, and the recent geopolitical jitters, their account would be worth approximately $71,000 (assuming S&P 500 returns), a 47.9% gain.

Someone who tried to time entries and exits? Academic research and broker data show they typically underperform by 3-6% annually, eaten alive by transaction costs, taxes on short-term trades, and the impossibility of calling both the exit and re-entry correctly.

The expensive shares you buy today are only "expensive" if prices never go higher. Given that the long-term trend of diversified equity markets has been up roughly 10% annually, buying at all-time highs has historically been fine—as long as you kept buying when prices fell, too.

## Real-world execution: automate or fail

The gap between understanding DCA and actually doing it consistently is where most people fail.

Melissa Park, 29, earns $52,000 as an elementary teacher in suburban Milwaukee. She opened a Roth IRA in 2022 and successfully contributed $200 monthly for eight months. Then summer hit, paychecks paused, and she "forgot" to restart contributions in September. By December, she felt like she'd already missed too much and would start fresh in January. She didn't.

Compare that to her colleague David, who earns $54,000. He set up automatic transfers: $200 moves from checking to his Roth IRA on the 3rd of every month, then auto-invests into a target-date fund. He's never missed a contribution in three years. He couldn't tell you what the market did last month.

Automation removes willpower from the equation. You're not deciding whether to invest each month—you're deciding once, then letting the system execute.

Practical steps:

1. **Set the contribution amount based on your budget**, not market conditions. If you can sustainably invest $300 monthly, commit to that. Not $300 "when the market dips."

2. **Choose the same day each month**—ideally right after payday. Investing on the 1st vs. the 15th doesn't matter; consistency does.

3. **Auto-invest into a diversified, low-cost fund**. A total market index fund (like VTSAX or FSKAX) or a target-date fund eliminates the need to also time which investments to buy.

4. **Turn off price alerts and stop checking daily**. If you're investing for 20+ years, today's price is essentially irrelevant.

5. **Commit to never pausing for market timing**. You can pause for genuine financial emergencies (job loss, medical crisis). Not for "the market feels high."

## When volatility surges: the opportunity hiding in fear

[CNBC: Supply overhang in the bond market and Middle East conflict could heighten volatility](https://www.cnbc.com/video/2026/07/22/oversupply-and-middle-east-conflict-could-spur-bond-market-volatility.html) discusses current factors that worry strategists. These same factors make headlines scream danger.

They also create the exact environment where DCA shines.

When the VIX (the market's "fear gauge") spikes, share prices typically drop. [CNBC: The VIX at the 15-16 level and dips in memory stocks are good buys](https://www.cnbc.com/video/2026/07/17/vix-at-15-16-level-and-dips-in-memory-stocks-are-good-buys-stutland.html) notes that some portfolio managers view moderate volatility as a buying opportunity—but most retail investors experience it as panic.

If you're DCA-ing, you don't need to view it as anything. Your $500 simply buys more shares. The decision was already made.

Consider running this thought experiment: Look back at the last time you felt scared about the market. Maybe March 2020, when COVID crashed everything. Maybe October 2022, when inflation fears drove a bear market. Now imagine you had simply kept contributing $400 monthly through that entire period. You'd have bought shares at depressed prices, and when the market recovered (as it always has), those shares would have appreciated alongside everything else.

The investors who profit from scary markets are the ones who keep buying. Not because they're brilliant, but because they have a process that continues even when headlines scream "SELL."

## What DCA doesn't do (and when you need more)

Let's be clear about limitations:

**DCA doesn't protect you from a bad investment.** If you're dollar-cost averaging into a single stock (even a "safe" one like a mega-cap tech company) or a narrow sector ETF, you're still taking concentrated risk. DCA into diversified index funds, not speculation.

**DCA doesn't replace asset allocation.** A 25-year-old can probably handle 90-100% stocks. A 60-year-old needs bonds and other stabilizers. DCA is about consistent contributions, not about what you're contributing into. Work with a CFP if you're unsure about appropriate asset allocation for your age and risk tolerance.

**DCA doesn't beat lump-sum investing on average.** If you suddenly receive $50,000 (inheritance, bonus, windfall), studies show that investing it immediately outperforms spreading it out over 12 months about 66% of the time. That's because markets trend up. But DCA is designed for regular income, not lump sums.

**DCA can't fix an inadequate savings rate.** If you're contributing $100 monthly on a $75,000 salary, DCA will execute perfectly—and you'll still fall far short of retirement goals. Aim for 15-20% of gross income (including employer match) if you're starting in your 20s or 30s.

## The tax and account structure questions

Where you DCA matters almost as much as that you DCA.

**Roth IRA**: Contributions up to $7,000 annually (2026 limits) if you're under 50. Your money grows tax-free forever, and qualified withdrawals in retirement are tax-free. Ideal for younger investors who expect higher income later.

**Traditional 401(k)**: Contributions are pre-tax, reducing your current taxable income. Your investments grow tax-deferred, but withdrawals in retirement are taxed as ordinary income. Makes sense if you're in a high tax bracket now.

**Taxable brokerage**: No contribution limits and no early withdrawal penalties, but you'll owe capital gains tax when you sell. Useful after maxing tax-advantaged accounts or if you're saving for a goal before retirement age.

For most people, the priority order is:
1. Contribute to 401(k) up to employer match (free money)
2. Max Roth IRA ($7,000)
3. Return to 401(k) and max it ($23,500 limit)
4. Taxable brokerage for anything beyond that

DCA works in all three. But the tax treatment dramatically affects your after-tax returns over decades. If you're contributing $500 monthly and in the 24% federal bracket, routing that through a traditional 401(k) saves you $1,440 annually in taxes vs. a taxable account.

These are complex enough that you should consult a CPA or CFP to optimize for your specific situation—especially if you have student loans, high-interest debt, or irregular income.

## The psychological advantage nobody talks about

There's a sneaky benefit to DCA that has nothing to do with math: it keeps you engaged with investing without requiring constant decisions.

When you check your portfolio and see gains, you feel smart. When you check and see losses, you feel foolish. Both emotions are traps.

DCA reframes the experience. You're not checking whether you made the "right" call. You're watching a process unfold. Down months mean your next contribution buys more shares. Up months mean your existing shares are worth more. Both are fine.

This psychological reframe is what allows investors like David (the teacher who automated everything) to stay invested through corrections that scare everyone else out. He's not immune to fear—he just doesn't have to make a decision when he's afraid.

## Case study: 15 years of DCA through three crises

Let's track a realistic long-term example.

Rachel Kim started her career in 2010 at age 25, earning $42,000 as a junior accountant. She committed to contributing $250 monthly to her Roth IRA, invested in a total U.S. stock market index fund.

By 2026, she's 41, earning $81,000, and has increased her contribution to $500 monthly (still manageable on her higher salary).

Over those 16 years, she invested through:
- The 2011 debt ceiling crisis and subsequent market drop
- The 2015-2016 slowdown and oil price crash
- The 2020 COVID crash (roughly -34% in one month)
- The 2022 inflation-driven bear market (-25% peak to trough)
- Multiple "geopolitical concerns" and volatility spikes

She never paused contributions. She never tried to time a re-entry. Her account experienced every one of those drops—and every subsequent recovery.

After 16 years of contributions totaling approximately $73,500 (accounting for her increases), her account sits at roughly $168,000 (assuming the historical S&P 500 average of ~10% annual returns, with realistic volatility).

Someone who tried to time the market—pausing during the scary periods and resuming after recoveries—would have missed significant portions of the recoveries (which tend to happen fast) and likely ended up with 20-30% less wealth.

Rachel's not a genius. She's not even particularly interested in investing. She's just disciplined and automated.

## When to actually stop DCA-ing

There are legitimate reasons to pause or stop:

**Job loss or income disruption**: If you lose your job, stopping retirement contributions to preserve cash for rent and food is rational. Build your emergency fund back up, then restart contributions.

**High-interest debt**: If you're carrying credit card balances at 22% APR, paying those off delivers a guaranteed 22% "return." Max out any employer 401(k) match (that's still free money), then attack the debt. Resume full contributions once it's cleared.

**Near-term major expense**: If you're buying a house in six months and need every dollar for the down payment, that's a legitimate reason to pause retirement contributions temporarily. But "near-term" means months, not years.

**Shifting to lump-sum investing**: If your income becomes irregular (you start a business, move into commission-based sales, etc.), you might shift from monthly DCA to quarterly or annual contributions. That's still a process—just a different cadence.

Notice what's not on this list: "The market feels expensive," "analysts predict a correction," or "geopolitical tensions are high."

## Key takeaways

- Dollar-cost averaging removes the need to time the market by investing fixed amounts at regular intervals, automatically buying more shares when prices drop and fewer when they rise.

- The real challenge isn't understanding DCA—it's continuing to execute when markets feel scary and every instinct screams to stop buying.

- Automation is essential; investors who manually decide each month whether to contribute fail at far higher rates than those who set up auto-transfers and auto-investing.

- DCA works best with diversified, low-cost index funds in tax-advantaged accounts (Roth IRA, 401(k)), not single stocks or sector bets.

- Volatility and market drops are when DCA delivers its greatest advantage, allowing you to accumulate shares at lower prices that appreciate during recoveries.
