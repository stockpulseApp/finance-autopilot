An emergency fund is not an investment — it is **insurance you pay yourself**. It buys time when income drops, a car dies, or medical bills hit. This blueprint shows how to size it, where to hold it, and how to automate deposits so willpower is irrelevant.

---

## How much is enough?

Use **essential monthly expenses**, not gross income:

```
Essentials = housing + utilities + food + transport + insurance
           + minimum debt payments + required childcare
```

| Stage | Target | Who it fits |
|-------|--------|-------------|
| **Starter** | $1,000–$2,500 | While paying high-APR debt or building habit |
| **Stable** | 3 months essentials | Dual income, W-2, affordable housing |
| **Cautious** | 6 months essentials | Commission income, single earner, health volatility |
| **Self-employed** | 6–12 months | Irregular invoices, no short-term disability |

**Do not** count invested retirement money as emergency cash — selling in a downturn can crystallize losses. Keep liquid cash separate.

---

## Where to park it

Requirements:
1. **FDIC or NCUA insured** (check bank/credit union)
2. **Separate from checking** — reduces accidental spending
3. **Same-day or next-day transfer** to checking when needed
4. Competitive **APY** — shop annually, rates move with Fed policy

Avoid:
- Stocks or crypto for emergencies
- CDs with penalties if you might need money in 30 days (ladder later)
- Checking accounts paying 0.01% when HYSA pays more

### Rate shopping framework

| Factor | Why it matters |
|--------|----------------|
| APY | Higher = faster growth on idle cash |
| Transfer speed | Emergencies are time-sensitive |
| Mobile app | You will monitor less if it is easy |
| Intro bonuses | Fine if you meet terms; do not chase forever |

## Build order: cash vs. debt vs. invest

**If credit card APR > ~8%:**  
Build starter fund ($1k) → avalanche high-APR debt → full emergency target → invest beyond match.

**If low-rate mortgage/student debt and stable job:**  
Starter fund → capture 401(k) match → split extra between emergency fill and IRA.

There is no moral prize for being debt-free before a $500 buffer — one flat tire should not become new card debt at 24% APR.

## Automation that actually sticks

### The payday rule
Schedule transfer for **day after deposit**, not “end of month” when account is empty.

Example on $4,200 take-home:
- Month 1–2: $100 to emergency HYSA
- Month 3–4: $150
- Month 5+: 10% of take-home until target hit

### Percentage escalation
Each quarter, raise auto-transfer by **1% of pay** until you hit target. You adapt slowly; lifestyle inflation fights back less.

### Named accounts
Label savings “Emergency — do not touch” in the app. Psychological friction works.

## When to use it (and when not to)

**Valid emergencies:** job loss, medical deductible, essential car repair, urgent travel for family crisis.

**Not emergencies:** sales, vacations, gifts, upgrading phone, investing “the dip.”

If you raid the fund for non-emergencies, **repay yourself** with a written plan within 90 days — same discipline as paying back a family loan.

---

## Replenishment protocol

After a withdrawal:
1. Pause optional investing 1–2 months if needed  
2. Set temporary higher auto-transfer  
3. Log the reason — patterns (old car, leaky roof) inform insurance or sinking funds  

Create **sinking funds** for predictable irregulars: car maintenance, annual insurance, holidays. Emergency fund stays for surprises only.

---

## Taxes and reporting

HYSA interest is taxable. You may receive 1099-INT. No special tax advantage for vanilla savings — that is why we do not over-save here forever.

HSA can double as medical emergency reserve *if* you understand HDHP rules and reimbursement documentation.

## Worked example: Jordan, 29

- Take-home: $3,800/mo  
- Essentials: $2,400/mo  
- Target: 3 months = **$7,200**  
- Starter done: $1,500 in HYSA  
- Auto-transfer: $200/paycheck (biweekly) = ~$433/mo  

Months to full fund from $1,500: (~$5,700) / $433 ≈ **13 months** alongside minimum debt payments.

Jordan keeps $400 in checking buffer so overdraft never triggers a $35 fee.
